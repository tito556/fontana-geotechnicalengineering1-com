SPT (Standard Penetration Test) in Fontana: Soil Profiling for Seismic Site Class

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

LEARN MORE →

Fontana sits on the distal slopes of the Lytle Creek and Cajon Wash alluvial fans, where granular layers interfinger with fine-grained flood-basin deposits—a stratigraphy that changes drastically within 200 feet of lateral distance. This depositional complexity amplifies the risk of differential settlement and, more critically, liquefaction under seismic shaking, given the city’s location within the Inland Empire’s high-hazard corridor. A properly executed SPT program cuts through that uncertainty. Our field crews operate a CME-75 automatic hammer system calibrated to ASTM D1586-18 energy standards, recovering split-spoon samples at 5-foot intervals while recording blow counts that feed directly into liquefaction potential analysis and bearing capacity models. The N60 values we obtain also support CPT correlations when clients need continuous soil behavior type profiling alongside discrete sample recovery.

N60 values below 15 in saturated sandy silts at Fontana routinely trigger liquefaction mitigation requirements under IBC Chapter 18.

Methodology and scope

ASTM D1586-18 governs every step of our Fontana operations, but referencing the standard alone does not capture the local reality: hammer efficiency corrections here must account for the variable groundwater table—often found between 15 and 40 feet depth depending on the season and proximity to the Santa Ana River pressure ridge. We log each borehole per ASTM D2488/USCS field classification, measuring recovery ratio, sample disturbance, and SPT refusal depth where cobble-rich paleochannel deposits appear. These refusal zones are common in Fontana’s mid-depth profile and frequently signal the top of the older alluvial fan surface. For projects requiring stiffness profiling across broader areas, the SPT grid can be combined with MASW surface-wave testing to generate Vs profiles that satisfy ASCE 7-22 site classification requirements without drilling every parcel. Our lab then processes the recovered samples through grain-size distribution analysis, providing the fines content and gradation parameters needed for liquefaction triggering correlations using the NCEER/Youd-Idriss framework.

Local considerations

Fontana’s growth from a 1913 agricultural colony into a logistics and suburban hub placed heavy infrastructure—warehouses, overpasses, residential tracts—directly atop Holocene-age alluvium that had never been engineered for modern structural loads. The 1971 San Fernando and 1992 Landers/Big Bear sequences demonstrated that deep basin effects can amplify ground motion well beyond what rock-site hazard maps predict, and the San Jacinto fault lies less than 8 miles from the city center. SPT data forms the backbone of liquefaction hazard assessment because the N-value, corrected for fines content and overburden stress, is the primary input to the simplified procedure that determines factor of safety against pore-pressure buildup. When our lab reports (N1)60cs values below 20 in saturated sands between 10 and 35 feet depth, the geotechnical engineer must either design ground improvement or deep foundations—a decision that carries cost implications reaching into the hundreds of thousands of dollars for a typical tilt-up distribution center.

Associated technical services

01

SPT with Laboratory Index Testing Package

Every borehole includes moisture content, Atterberg limits (ASTM D4318), and sieve analysis (ASTM D6913) on selected split-spoon samples. This package converts raw blow counts into USCS classifications and provides the plasticity index and fines content required for liquefaction triggering curves. Turnaround is typically five business days from rig demobilization.

02

Liquefaction & Seismic Site Class Report

We integrate SPT N60 profiles with groundwater data to compute (N1)60cs, cyclic stress ratio, and factor of safety per depth increment. The deliverable includes a site class letter per ASCE 7-22 Table 20.3-1, suitable for structural engineer's foundation design package and plan-check review by the City of Fontana Building & Safety Division.

Frequently asked questions

How much does an SPT (Standard Penetration Test) boring cost in Fontana?

For a typical 30-foot borehole in Fontana's alluvial soils, SPT drilling ranges from US$520 to US$680 per hole, which includes the drill rig with two-person crew, split-spoon sampling at 5-foot intervals, field logging, and a summary boring log. Deeper borings, weekend work, or sites requiring traffic control may increase the unit cost.

What depth do SPT borings typically go to in Fontana for a commercial building?

Per IBC 2021 Section 1803, borings must extend through unsuitable strata and at least 30 feet below the foundation level, or to refusal. In Fontana, most commercial projects require 30- to 50-foot borings; deeper holes are needed when the preliminary geotechnical investigation identifies potentially liquefiable layers that extend below 40 feet.

How do you correct SPT N-values for hammer energy and overburden?

We apply the ASTM D1586/D4633 framework: raw N is corrected to N60 using the hammer's calibrated energy ratio (ER=82% for our automatic hammer), then normalized to (N1)60 for vertical effective stress using the Liao-Whitman overburden correction factor. For liquefaction, we further adjust to (N1)60cs using the Idriss-Boulanger fines-content correction.

What triggers an SPT refusal condition, and what does it mean for the project?

Refusal is defined as 50 blows for 6 inches of penetration or 100 total blows on the split-spoon. In Fontana, refusal typically occurs when encountering the dense cobble layer associated with the older alluvial fan surface or granitic bedrock of the San Gabriel foothills. Refusal at shallow depth may require switching to rock coring or revising the foundation type to a deep system.

How soon can you mobilize an SPT drill rig in Fontana?

Standard mobilization is five to seven business days after permit and utility clearance. We hold a current City of Fontana business license and maintain pre-qualified traffic control subcontractors for sites along Sierra Avenue or Valley Boulevard corridors, which helps avoid delays during the encroachment permit stage.
